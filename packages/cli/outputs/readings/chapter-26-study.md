# Reading 26: Consecration

## Introduction

Consecration is the act of setting oneself apart wholly for God's service and glory. In the Old Testament, priests were anointed and set apart for sacred ministry; in the New Testament, every believer is called to this same holy separation. True consecration is not mere religious sentimentâ€”it is a complete surrender of all we have and are to Christ, that He might live His life through us and make us partakers of His divine nature.

## Core Truths

### A Royal Priesthood Called Out

> _"But ye are a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, a peculiar people; that ye should shew forth the praises of Him who hath called you out of darkness into His marvellous light."_ (1 Peter 2:9)

Peter reminds us that before this calling, we "were not a people, but are now the people of God" (1 Peter 2:10). ### The Public Commitment of Baptism

## Deeper Truths

## Principles and Great Controversy Themes

### The Sanctuary and Consecration

In the sanctuary service, consecration was vividly illustrated. The priest could not serve until he was anointed with holy oil (Exodus 30:30)â€”a symbol of the Holy Spirit's indwelling. He must wash at the laver before entering the holy place (Exodus 30:19â€“21)â€”symbolizing cleansing from sin. Only then could he minister before God.

So today, we cannot serve acceptably until we are anointed by the Spirit, cleansed by the blood of Christ, and set apart for holy use. The sanctuary teaches that consecration is not optionalâ€”it is the **prerequisite** for spiritual ministry.

- **The Outer Court (Altar of Sacrifice)**: We come to Christ, laying our sins upon Him who died for us. This is justificationâ€”acceptance through His merits.
- **The Laver**: We are washed, cleansed, made ready for service.
- **The Holy Place**: Daily ministryâ€”living in the light of God's Word, fed by the Bread of Life, offering the incense of prayer.
- **The Most Holy Place**: The deepest consecrationâ€”where nothing is withheld, where Christ's righteousness is fully imparted, where the believer is prepared for translation.

### Righteousness by Faith and Consecration

The call to consecration is not a call to human self-effort. We cannot make ourselves holy by willpower or religious discipline. The promises of God are given "that by these ye might be partakers of the divine nature, having escaped the corruption that is in the world through lust" (2 Peter 1:4).

**Partaking of the divine nature** is not human achievementâ€”it is divine gift received by faith. We surrender; Christ transforms. We yield; He empowers. We abide; He produces fruit.

[Q] **"But doesn't this sound like works? Isn't salvation by grace alone?"**

Consecration is not the **cause** of salvation but the **response** to it. We are saved by grace through faith (Ephesians 2:8). But that saving grace transforms us. The branch that abides in the vine **will** bear fruitâ€”not to earn acceptance, but because life flows from the Source. A consecrated life is the natural result of a living connection with Christ, not a human effort to earn God's favor.

## Practical Application

This truth calls us to:

1. **Acknowledge that we belong to God.** "Ye are not your own; for ye are bought with a price: therefore glorify God in your body, and in your spirit, which are God's" (1 Corinthians 6:19â€“20). Our bodies are temples of the Holy Spiritâ€”how we eat, drink, dress, speak, and live matters.

3. **Abide daily in Christ.** Consecration is not a one-time event but a daily, hourly dependence upon Christ. Each morning, place yourself anew upon the altar. Each moment, choose to remain connected to the Vine.

4. **Stand ready for service.** When God calls, the consecrated soul responds as Isaiah did: "Here am I; send me" (Isaiah 6:8). The eyes of the true servant are ever upon the Master's hand, watching for direction (Psalm 123:2).

5. **Expect opposition and rejoice in it.** If the world hates you, remember that it hated Christ first (John 15:18). Persecution is the badge of true discipleship.

[ILL] A servant in a great house does not fix his eyes on the guests, the decorations, or the feast. His eyes are upon his master's hand, watching for the slightest gestureâ€”ready instantly to obey. So the consecrated Christian watches the hand of his Lord, ready at a moment's notice to go, to speak, to serve, to wait. This is the spirit of true consecration.
