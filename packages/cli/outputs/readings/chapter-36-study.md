# Reading 36: The Sufferings of Christ

## Introduction

The cross of Calvary stands at the center of human historyâ€”the moment when infinite love met infinite suffering. Yet many Christians pass by the scenes of Christ's agony with mere intellectual acknowledgment, never pausing to grasp the depths of what our Saviour endured. To understand His sufferings is to understand the weight of sin, the character of God, and the price of our redemption. This study invites you to walk with Christ through His darkest hours, that your heart may be forever changed by what love has done.

## Core Truths

### Why Christ Came

The mission of Jesus was singular and clear: _"Christ Jesus came into the world to save sinners"_ (1 Timothy 1:15). He did not come to establish an earthly kingdom, reform political systems, or merely teach moral philosophy. He came to rescue a perishing race.

What moved the Father to send His Son into such suffering?

> "For God so loved the world, that He gave His only begotten Son, that whosoever believeth in Him should not perish, but have everlasting life." (John 3:16)

- **Love initiated the plan**â€”not our merit or worthiness
- **Love sustained the sacrifice**â€”through every blow and insult
- **Love completed the work**â€”even unto death

Paul emphasizes this motivation: _"God commendeth His love toward us, in that, while we were yet sinners, Christ died for us"_ (Romans 5:8). We were not lovable; we were loved. This is the gospel.

### The Lamb Foretold

Seven centuries before Bethlehem, Isaiah painted the portrait of a suffering Messiah:

> "He was oppressed, and He was afflicted, yet He opened not His mouth: He is brought as a lamb to the slaughter, and as a sheep before her shearers is dumb, so He openeth not His mouth." (Isaiah 53:7)

Notice the imagery: **a lamb**. This connects directly to the sanctuary service, where lambs were slain daily for sin. Every bleeding sacrifice pointed forward to this momentâ€”when the true Lamb of God would take away the sin of the world (John 1:29).

Christ knew exactly what awaited Him. He told His disciples plainly:

> "Behold, we go up to Jerusalem, and all things that are written by the prophets concerning the Son of man shall be accomplished. He walked toward the cross with open eyes. This was no accident, no tragedy that caught heaven by surprise. It was the eternal plan of redemption unfolding in time.

## Deeper Truths

### Gethsemane: The Weight of a World's Sin

The physical torture of crucifixion, though terrible, was not the deepest suffering of Christ. His agony began in the Garden of Gethsemane, where something far worse than nails and thorns pressed upon His soul.

> "My soul is exceeding sorrowful, even unto death: tarry ye here, and watch with Me." (Matthew 26:38)

What caused this overwhelming sorrow? The sins of the worldâ€”your sins, my sins, the accumulated guilt of every human being who ever livedâ€”were being laid upon the sinless Son of God. For the first time in eternity, Christ experienced the horror of sin's presence as His own.

> "And being in an agony He prayed more earnestly: and His sweat was as it were great drops of blood falling down to the ground." (Luke 22:44)

His prayer reveals the essence of surrender:

> "O My Father, if it be possible, let this cup pass from Me: nevertheless not as I will, but as Thou wilt." (Matthew 26:39)

The "cup" was the cup of God's wrath against sin (Jeremiah 25:15; Revelation 14:10). Christ shrank from itâ€”not because of cowardice, but because His pure nature recoiled from contact with the vileness of sin. Yet He drank it to the dregs for us.

## Principles and Great Controversy Themes

### The Character of God Revealed

The cross answers the great controversy's central question: What is God really like?

Satan had charged God with being arbitrary, severe, and unforgiving. The cross proves otherwise. Here we see:

- **Love** that gives everything
- **Justice** that cannot overlook sin
- **Mercy** that provides a substitute
- **Humility** that stoops to the lowest place

_"Hereby perceive we the love of God, because He laid down His life for us"_ (1 John 3:16). The cross is God's final argument against Satan's lies.

### The Sanctuary Unveiled

The earthly sanctuary was a shadow; Calvary was the substance.

- **The altar of burnt offering** (outer court): Christ's body broken for us
- **The laver**: His blood cleansing from all sin
- **The veil torn**: Access to God restored
- **The High Priest's sacrifice**: He offered Himself, once for all (Hebrews 9:26)

But the work did not end at the cross. The Lamb became the High Priest. He ascended to the heavenly sanctuary, where He now ministers His blood on our behalf (Hebrews 8:1â€“2; 9:24). The benefits of Calvary must be _applied_ as well as _provided_.

[Q] **"If Christ's sacrifice was 'finished,' why does He still need to minister in heaven?"**

The sacrifice was completeâ€”nothing can be added to it. But the _application_ of that sacrifice continues. A debt may be paid in full, yet the debtor must still receive the receipt. Christ's heavenly ministry applies His merits to repentant sinners, cleanses the sanctuary of the record of confessed sins, and prepares a people to stand before a holy God. The cross made salvation _possible_; the high priestly ministry makes it _actual_ in individual lives.

### Righteousness by Faith at the Cross

At Calvary, we see both the impossibility of human self-salvation and the all-sufficiency of Christ.

- We contributed **nothing** to our redemption but the sin that made it necessary
- Christ provided **everything**: perfect obedience, atoning sacrifice, risen life
- We receive His righteousness **by faith alone**â€”trusting wholly in His merits

_"He hath made Him to be sin for us, who knew no sin; that we might be made the righteousness of God in Him"_ (2 Corinthians 5:21).

This exchange is the heart of the gospel. Our sin was imputed to Christ; His righteousness is imputed to us. And through the Holy Spirit, His character is _imparted_ to us as we behold Him by faith.

[ILL] A condemned criminal stands before the king. The sentence is death. But the king's own son steps forward: "Let his punishment fall on me." The son dies in the criminal's place, and the prisoner walks freeâ€”not because of anything he did, but solely because of what the son did for him. This is justification by faith: we stand acquitted because Another took our sentence.

## Practical Application

### Beholding Transforms

_"We all, with open face beholding as in a glass the glory of the Lord, are changed into the same image"_ (2 Corinthians 3:18).

The sufferings of Christ are not merely historical facts to be memorized; they are spiritual realities to be contemplated. As we meditate upon the cross:

- **Pride melts** before such humility
- **Self-pity fades** before such suffering
- **Grudges dissolve** before such forgiveness
- **Coldness warms** before such love

Make it a daily practice to spend time at the cross. Read the passion narratives slowly. Ask the Holy Spirit to open your eyes. Let the scenes become vivid and personal. Christ did this _for you_.

### Living the Crucified Life

Paul declared, _"I am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me"_ (Galatians 2:20).

What does this mean practically?

1. **Death to self-will**: "Not as I will, but as Thou wilt" becomes our daily prayer
2. **Death to self-defense**: We no longer fight for our rights or reputation
3. **Death to self-indulgence**: The cross calls us to sacrifice, not comfort
4. **Life in Christ**: His resurrection power enables what our flesh cannot do

Victory over sin is not achieved by human effort but by continual surrender to the crucified and risen Saviour. We cannot save ourselves, but He can save usâ€”fully, completely, eternally.

### The Cross and Future Trial

A time of trouble is coming upon the earth such as never was (Daniel 12:1). God's people will face persecution, deprivation, and death. How shall they endure?

By looking unto Jesus, _"the author and finisher of our faith; who for the joy that was set before Him endured the cross, despising the shame"_ (Hebrews 12:2).

If the Son of God could endure Gethsemane and Calvary for us, can we not endure our lesser trials for Him? His sufferings were redemptive; ours are merely disciplinary. His were infinite; ours are measured. His love carries us through.

## Appeal

Will you come?
