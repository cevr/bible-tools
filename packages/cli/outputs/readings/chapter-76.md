# Reading 76: The Two Covenants

**Central truth**: The Bible describes two covenants — two fundamentally different approaches to the problem of sin. One is built on humanity's promise to obey God. The other is built on God's promise to transform humanity. One always fails. The other never has. The surprise: the one that works is far older than the one that fails.

---

## The Experience Everyone Knows

- **The cycle of resolve and failure**
  - "I'll do better tomorrow." "This time I'll really change." "I promise I won't do that again."
  - Every human being knows this pattern: sincere commitment followed by inevitable failure
  - We promise. We break the promise. We promise harder. We break it again.

- **The Bible has a name for this pattern**
  - Scripture calls it the "old covenant" — not because it belongs to an ancient era, but because it is the oldest human instinct: trying to fix ourselves by the strength of our own resolve
  - And the Bible has a name for the alternative: the "new covenant" — God's promise to do in us what we cannot do ourselves

- **Two foundations, same law**
  - Both covenants involve God's moral law — the Ten Commandments
  - The difference is not _what_ must be done but _who does it_
  - Old covenant: "We will obey" (human promise → human power)
  - New covenant: "I will transform you" (God's promise → God's power)

- **The surprise**
  - Most people assume the old covenant came first and God upgraded to the new
  - The Bible tells a different story: God's promise — the "new" covenant — is actually the oldest covenant in Scripture. It appears in Eden, in the first chapter after the Fall. The "old" covenant — humanity's promise — is the interruption that came later.

- This reading traces both covenants from their origin and asks: which foundation are you standing on?

---

## In the Beginning: God's "I Will"

- **The setting: Eden after the Fall**
  - Humanity has just sinned for the first time. Adam and Eve are hiding, ashamed, exposed.
  - Notice who acts first: Adam hides. God seeks. Adam covers himself with fig leaves (Gen. 3:7). God provides coats of skins (Gen. 3:21). Adam blames. God promises.
  - Before any human resolution, before any vow to do better — God speaks.

- **The first covenant promise in Scripture**

  > **Genesis 3:15** — "I will put enmity between thee and the woman, and between thy seed and her seed; it shall bruise thy head, and thou shalt bruise his heel."
  - Five words define this covenant: **"I will put enmity"**
  - God doesn't ask Adam to generate hatred for sin — He promises to put it there
  - God doesn't ask Adam to crush the serpent — He promises the Seed will do it
  - This is the "new" covenant in its first form: God acting, humanity receiving

- **The skins confirm the pattern**

  > **Genesis 3:21** — "Unto Adam also and to his wife did the LORD God make coats of skins, and clothed them."
  - A covering provided by God through sacrifice, not human weaving
  - The fig leaves = humanity's first attempt at self-covering — sincere but useless
  - The skins = God's covenant in action: covering requires death, substitution, divine initiative
  - [→] even in Eden, two approaches are visible: man's effort (fig leaves) and God's provision (skins)

- **The foundation is laid**
  - God promises. God provides. God covers.
  - Humanity's role: receive, trust, cooperate — not originate.
  - This is the covenant. Everything that follows is either a continuation of it or an interruption.

[DYK🔎]

> **Hebrews 13:8** — "Jesus Christ the same yesterday, and to day, and for ever."

If God never changes, He didn't start with law and switch to grace. He started with grace.

## The Pattern Emerges: Cain, Abel, and Abraham

- **Two brothers, two approaches — immediately**
  > **Genesis 4:3–5** — "And in process of time it came to pass, that Cain brought of the fruit of the ground an offering unto the LORD. And Abel, he also brought of the firstlings of his flock and of the fat thereof. And the LORD had respect unto Abel and to his offering: But unto Cain and to his offering he had not respect. And Cain was very wroth, and his countenance fell."
  - Cain: fruit of the ground — the labor of his own hands, no blood, no substitute
  - Abel: firstlings of the flock — faith in God's provision, trusting the Substitute promised in Genesis 3:15
  - Both worship. Both bring offerings. Both are sincere. Only one is accepted.
  - The difference: Cain's offering says "I will provide for myself." Abel's says "God will provide the Lamb."
  - [→] the two covenants are already visible in the first generation after Eden — not as theology, but as lived experience

[ILL] Like paying a real debt with homemade play money — no matter how carefully printed, it has no value. Only the King's currency (Christ's righteousness) is accepted.

- **Abraham: receiving the covenant, then interrupting it**
  - God promises Abraham a son. Abraham believes. It is counted as righteousness (Gen. 15:6).
    > **Romans 4:20–22** — "He staggered not at the promise of God through unbelief; but was strong in faith, giving glory to God; And being fully persuaded that, what he had promised, he was able also to perform. And therefore it was imputed to him for righteousness."
  - This is the original covenant at work: God promises, Abraham receives, faith is enough.

- **Then the interruption — even Abraham falls into it**

  > **Genesis 16:1–2** — "Now Sarai Abram's wife bare him no children: and she had an handmaid, an Egyptian, whose name was Hagar. And Sarai said unto Abram, Behold now, the LORD hath restrained me from bearing: I pray thee, go in unto my maid; it may be that I may obtain children by her. And Abram hearkened to the voice of Sarai."
  - Ishmael = the product of flesh trying to fulfill God's promise by human devising
  - The promise was "I will give you a son." Abraham and Sarah heard it and said, "Let us help God out."
  - Same pattern as the fig leaves: God promises, humanity takes over.

- **God reasserts the original**

  > **Genesis 21:1–2** — "And the LORD visited Sarah as he had said, and the LORD did unto Sarah as he had spoken. For Sarah conceived, and bare Abraham a son in his old age, at the set time of which God had spoken to him."
  - Isaac = impossible by human strength, purely miracle of God's word
  - Born when Abraham's body was "as good as dead" (Rom. 4:19) — so no one could claim credit

- **Paul names the pattern explicitly**
  > **Galatians 4:22–26** — "For it is written, that Abraham had two sons, the one by a bondmaid, the other by a freewoman. But he who was of the bondwoman was born after the flesh; but he of the freewoman was by promise. Which things are an allegory: for these are the two covenants; the one from the mount Sinai, which gendereth to bondage, which is Agar. For this Agar is mount Sinai in Arabia, and answereth to Jerusalem which now is, and is in bondage with her children. But Jerusalem which is above is free, which is the mother of us all."
  - Hagar → flesh → bondage → "I can help God out"
  - Sarah → promise → freedom → "God must do the impossible in me"
  - The two covenants are not just doctrines — they are lived patterns, visible in every generation

[Q] **Can a person be sincere, religious, and industrious like Cain — yet still lost because the foundation is self instead of the Lamb?**
→ Yes. Sincerity is not the issue. The foundation is. Cain worked hard. Abraham schemed carefully. Both were earnest. But effort built on "I will do" cannot accomplish what only "God will do" can produce.

## The Great Interruption: Sinai

- Cain interrupted as an individual. Abraham and Sarah interrupted as a family. Now an entire nation does it.

- **God's reminder before the proposal**

  > **Exodus 19:3–4** — "Ye have seen what I did unto the Egyptians, and how I bare you on eagles' wings, and brought you unto Myself."
  - [→] everything up to this point has been "I will" — God acting, Israel receiving
  - Plagues: God's power. Red Sea: God's deliverance. Manna: God's provision. Pillar of fire: God's guidance.

- **The proposal**

  > **Exodus 19:5–6** — "Now therefore, if ye will obey My voice indeed, and keep My covenant, then ye shall be a peculiar treasure unto Me above all people: for all the earth is mine: And ye shall be unto Me a kingdom of priests, and an holy nation."

- **Then the interruption**

  > **Exodus 19:8** — "And all the people answered together, and said, All that the Lord hath spoken we will do. And Moses returned the words of the people unto the LORD."
  - Sounds loyal. But notice what shifted:
    - Up to this moment: "I will" — God acting
    - From this moment: "We will" — Israel promising
  - No "by Thy grace." No "God working in us." No acknowledgment of dependence.
  - They promised obedience before hearing the terms.

- **What was the covenant they were to keep?**

  > **Deuteronomy 4:12–13** — "He declared unto you His covenant, which He commanded you to perform, even ten commandments; and He wrote them upon two tables of stone."
  - The commandments existed before the agreement with Israel — they were not invented at Sinai
    > **Deuteronomy 5:22** — "And He added no more. And He wrote them in two tables of stone."

- **Ratification**
  - Moses wrote all the words (Ex. 24:4)
  - Read "the book of the covenant" to the people (Ex. 24:7)
  - People promised again:
    > **Exodus 24:7** — "All that the Lord hath said will we do, and be obedient."
  - Blood sprinkled on book and people (Ex. 24:8; Heb. 9:19, 20)
    > **Exodus 24:8** — "Behold the blood of the covenant, which the Lord hath made with you concerning all these words."

[Q] **What exactly is the "old covenant"?**
→ Think of it this way: God had already promised to save humanity by His own power (Gen. 3:15). At Sinai, Israel essentially said, "We don't need You to do it — we'll handle it ourselves." The old covenant is _humanity's promise to obey_ — the same law as Eden, but on a different foundation: man's word instead of God's. It is called "old" not because it came first in time (God's promise in Eden is older), but because it is obsolete by design — it was never meant to work. It was meant to show us we need the other one.

## Why the Interruption Failed

- **How long did the vow last?**

  > **Exodus 32:1** — "And when the people saw that Moses delayed to come down out of the mount, the people gathered themselves together unto Aaron, and said unto him, Up, make us gods, which shall go before us."
  - The vow shattered before Moses came down the mountain
  - Every covenant built on human willpower has the same shelf life

- **God's own diagnosis**

  > **Deuteronomy 5:28–29** — "And the LORD heard the voice of your words, when ye spake unto me; and the LORD said unto me, I have heard the voice of the words of this people, which they have spoken unto thee: they have well said all that they have spoken. O that there were such an heart in them, that they would fear Me, and keep all My commandments always, that it might be well with them, and with their children for ever!"
  - God says their words are "well said" — the desire is right
  - But He laments: "O that there were such a heart in them"
  - Their promise **exposes what they lack** — the very heart needed to keep it
  - This is the purpose of the interruption — not a different plan, but a diagnostic

- **The fatal flaw: the promises**

  > **Hebrews 8:7** — "If that first covenant had been faultless, then should no place have been sought for the second."
  > **Hebrews 8:6** — "He is the mediator of a better covenant, which was established upon better promises."
  - The fault was not in the law. It was in **who made the promise.**
    > **John 15:5** — "Without Me ye can do nothing."
    > **Isaiah 64:6** — "All our righteousnesses are as filthy rags."

- **Why God allowed it**
  > **Romans 3:20** — "By the law is the knowledge of sin."
  > **Romans 7:7** — "I had not known sin, but by the law."
  - Their promise + their failure = consciousness of guilt
  - Guilt → awareness of need → seeking help
  - God let them try so they would discover they could not — and turn back to what He offered in Eden

[ILL] Man drowning in ocean promises to swim to shore. The promise is sincere but powerless. What he needs is a rescuer, not a resolution. Sinai was the mirror that showed Israel their dirty face. It couldn't wash them — but it sent them looking for water.

## God Reasserts the Original

- After centuries of Israel's failure under "we will do," God speaks again through Jeremiah — and the language is unmistakable:

- **"I will make a new covenant"**

  > **Jeremiah 31:31** — "Behold, the days come, saith the Lord, that I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel, and with the house of Judah."
  > **Jeremiah 31:32** — "Not according to the covenant that I made with their fathers in the day that I took them by the hand to bring them out of the land of Egypt; which My covenant they brake, although I was an husband unto them, saith the LORD."
  - "Not according to the covenant... which **they** brake" — the interruption failed because it depended on them

- **Listen to the promises — and hear Eden again**

  > **Jeremiah 31:33** — "But this shall be the covenant that I will make with the house of Israel; After those days, saith the LORD, I will put my law in their inward parts, and write it in their hearts; and will be their God, and they shall be my people."
  > **Jeremiah 31:34** — "And they shall teach no more every man his neighbour, and every man his brother, saying, Know the LORD: for they shall all know me, from the least of them unto the greatest of them, saith the LORD: for I will forgive their iniquity, and I will remember their sin no more."
  - [→] count the "I will" statements. This is Genesis 3:15 language — expanded, but identical in structure
  - Eden: "I will put enmity." Jeremiah: "I will put My law in their hearts."
  - Same God. Same initiative. Same covenant.

- **Ezekiel confirms**

  > **Ezekiel 36:26–27** — "A new heart also will I give you, and a new spirit will I put within you: and I will take away the stony heart out of your flesh, and I will give you an heart of flesh. And I will put my spirit within you, and cause you to walk in my statutes, and ye shall keep my judgments, and do them."
  - Ezekiel answers Deuteronomy 5:29 directly:
    - God lamented: "O that there were such a heart in them"
    - God promises: "A new heart will I give you"
  - What they lacked, He will supply. What they could not do, He will do in them.

- **Jeremiah is not introducing something new. He is telling Israel: God is going back to the beginning.**

- **Order of fulfillment**
  1. Forgiveness of sins (must come first)
  2. Law written in heart (sanctification)
  3. Blotting out of sin (final atonement)
  4. All knowing the Lord (translation to heavenly Jerusalem)

[Q] **Is the law different in the new covenant?**
→ No — same Ten Commandments. The difference: God promises to write them _in the heart_, not just on stone. The law hasn't changed. The power source has — from "we will" to "I will."

## Christ Ratifies What Existed Since Eden

- **The covenant was operative by faith long before Calvary**

  > **Galatians 3:17** — "The covenant, that was confirmed before of God in Christ, the law, which was four hundred and thirty years after, cannot disannul, that it should make the promise of none effect."
  > **Galatians 3:29** — "If ye be Christ's, then are ye Abraham's seed, and heirs according to the promise."
  > **Galatians 3:7** — "They which are of faith, the same are the children of Abraham."
  - Abel, Abraham, Moses — all saved by faith in the promised Redeemer, not by their own vows

- **But a testament requires the death of the testator**

  > **Daniel 9:27** — "He shall confirm the covenant with many for one week: and in the midst of the week He shall cause the sacrifice and the oblation to cease."
  > **Hebrews 9:16–17** — "For where a testament is, there must also of necessity be the death of the testator. For a testament is of force after men are dead: otherwise it is of no strength at all while the testator liveth."
  - [→] Calvary doesn't create the covenant — it formally ratifies what existed since Eden

- **His death covers sins committed under the interruption**

  > **Hebrews 9:15** — "For this cause He is the mediator of the new testament, that by means of death, for the redemption of the transgressions that were under the first testament, they which are called might receive the promise of eternal inheritance."
  - [→] no forgiveness _by virtue of_ the old covenant — only through Christ

- **Every Old Testament sacrifice pointed to this**
  > **Hebrews 10:4** — "It is not possible that the blood of bulls and of goats should take away sins."
  - Every sacrifice was a confession: "I cannot save myself. I need the Lamb God promised in Eden."
  - The sacrificial system was never the old covenant — it was the original covenant's teaching tool

[TANGENT] Sanctuary connection: every sacrifice = acknowledgment that the sinner deserved death + faith in the coming Substitute promised in Genesis 3:15

## Righteousness by Faith: The Original Covenant Lived

- **The commandments must be kept**

  > **Matthew 19:17** — "If thou wilt enter into life, keep the commandments."
  > **Revelation 22:14** — "Blessed are they that do His commandments, that they may have right to the tree of life."

- **But only Christ can produce obedience — this was always the plan**

  > **John 15:5** — "Without Me ye can do nothing."
  > **Philippians 2:13** — "It is God which worketh in you both to will and to do of His good pleasure."
  > **Ephesians 2:10** — "We are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus unto good works, which God hath before ordained that we should walk in them."
  > **Hebrews 13:20–21** — "Now the God of peace... make you perfect in every good work to do His will, working in you that which is wellpleasing in His sight, through Jesus Christ."
  > **Galatians 2:20** — "I am crucified with Christ: nevertheless I live; yet not I, but Christ liveth in me: and the life which I now live in the flesh I live by the faith of the Son of God, who loved me, and gave himself for me."

- **Our part: yield, not strive**

  > **James 4:7** — "Submit yourselves therefore to God."
  > **Romans 6:13** — "Yield yourselves unto God, as those that are alive from the dead, and your members as instruments of righteousness unto God."
  - [→] this is what Abel understood at the altar, what Abraham learned through Isaac, and what Sinai's failure was designed to teach

- **The result: God's righteousness in us**
  > **Isaiah 54:17** — "Their righteousness is of Me, saith the Lord."
  > **Jeremiah 23:6** — "The Lord our Righteousness."
  > **Philippians 3:9** — "And be found in him, not having mine own righteousness, which is of the law, but that which is through the faith of Christ, the righteousness which is of God by faith."

[TANGENT] Most Holy Place work: Christ not only forgives but perfects a people who keep the commandments by His indwelling power — the final demonstration that the original covenant works, that God's "I will" produces what humanity's "we will" never could

## Summary: The Original and the Interruption

| Feature          | The Interruption (Sinai / "Old") | The Original (Eden–Calvary / "New") |
| ---------------- | -------------------------------- | ----------------------------------- |
| **Initiated**    | Sinai                            | Eden                                |
| **Foundation**   | "We will do" (man's promise)     | "I will" (God's promise)            |
| **Basis**        | Ten Commandments                 | Ten Commandments                    |
| **Power source** | Human effort                     | Christ in us                        |
| **Man's role**   | Promise to obey                  | Yield and receive                   |
| **God's role**   | Bless if obey                    | Transform, forgive, empower         |
| **Result**       | Failure, guilt                   | Victory, righteousness              |
| **Mediator**     | Moses                            | Christ                              |
| **Blood**        | Animal blood                     | Christ's blood                      |
| **Law location** | Stone tables                     | Heart                               |

## Appeal

- The question is not: "Will you try harder?"
- The question is: "Will you stop interrupting and let God finish what He started in Eden?"

- The covenant God made in Genesis 3:15 is still open
- Christ offers what Sinai could not give — power to obey from a transformed nature
- Every "we will do" ends in a golden calf. Every "I will" ends in a changed heart.

> **Romans 6:13** — "Yield yourselves unto God, as those that are alive from the dead."

> **Hebrews 8:10** — "I will put My laws into their mind, and write them in their hearts: and I will be to them a God, and they shall be to Me a people."

**The original covenant has always been: "I will." Will you stop interrupting — and let Him finish?**
