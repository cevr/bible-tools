# Bible Study: The Gentile Sabbath

## Introduction

## Core Truths

### God Is No Respecter of Persons

Scripture emphatically declares that God does not favor one nation over another in matters of salvation or worship:

> _"Is he the God of the Jews only? is he not also of the Gentiles? Yes, of the Gentiles also."_ (Romans 3:29)

> _"Of a truth I perceive that God is no respecter of persons: But in every nation he that feareth him, and worketh righteousness, is accepted with him."_ (Acts 10:34, 35)

If God shows no partiality between Jew and Gentile, then His moral lawâ€”including the Sabbath commandmentâ€”must apply equally to all. A God who is "the same yesterday, and to day, and for ever" (Hebrews 13:8) does not maintain one standard of holiness for one people and a different standard for another.

### The Sabbath Was Made for Man

Jesus Himself declared the universal scope of the Sabbath institution:

> _"The sabbath was made for man."_ (Mark 2:27)

Notice carefully: Christ did not say "the Sabbath was made for the Jew." He said it was made for **man**â€”_anthropos_ in Greek, meaning humanity, the human race. This same word appears in Genesis 1:27 (LXX) describing the creation of mankind. The Sabbath was instituted at creation (Genesis 2:1-3), centuries before any distinction between Jew and Gentile existed, before Abraham, before Moses, before Sinai.

**Key principle**: Whatever was established at creation for humanity belongs to all humanity for all time, unless Scripture explicitly removes it. God has never revoked the Sabbath; rather, He has consistently extended its blessings to all who would receive them.

## Deeper Truths

### The Law Convicts All Men of Sin

Paul establishes a crucial principle regarding God's moral law:

> _"For until the law sin was in the world: but sin is not imputed when there is no law."_ (Romans 5:13)

> _"Knowing this, that the law is not made for a righteous man, but for the lawless and disobedient, for the ungodly and for sinners... for menstealers, for liars, for perjured persons, and if there be any other thing that is contrary to sound doctrine."_ (1 Timothy 1:9, 10)

The Gentile world was filled with those who had "given themselves over unto lasciviousness, to work all uncleanness with greediness" (Ephesians 4:19). If sin cannot be imputed without law, and the Gentiles were clearly guilty of sin, then God's law must have applied to them. The fourth commandment stands inseparably united with the other nine.

[Q] **"But wasn't the law given only to Israel at Sinai?"**

The formal codification of the law at Sinai was indeed given to Israel, but the principles of that law existed from the beginning. Consider: Did murder become wrong only at Sinai? Was lying acceptable before Moses received the tablets? Cain was condemned for murder (Genesis 4), and lying brought judgment throughout Genesis. The law was "from the beginning" (1 John 3:8). Sinai was not the origin of God's law but its formal proclamation to a covenant people who would preserve and proclaim it to all nations.

### Strangers Included in Israel's Sabbath Observance

When Israel departed from Egypt, a "mixed multitude" accompanied them:

> _"And a mixed multitude went up also with them; and flocks, and herds, even very much cattle."_ (Exodus 12:38)

These were Egyptians and others who had seen God's mighty works and chose to leave with Israel. How did God regard them?

> _"One law shall be to him that is homeborn, and unto the stranger that sojourneth among you."_ (Exodus 12:49)

[DYKðŸ”Ž] The Hebrew word for "stranger" (_ger_) refers to a resident alienâ€”someone from another nation living among Israel. This term appears over 90 times in the Pentateuch alone, consistently showing God's concern for non-Israelites and His expectation that they would share in Israel's covenant blessings and responsibilities.

When the Sabbath commandment was formally given at Sinai, God explicitly named the stranger:

> _"The seventh day is the sabbath of the LORD thy God: in it thou shalt not do any work, thou, nor thy son, nor thy daughter, thy manservant, nor thy maidservant, nor thy cattle, nor **thy stranger that is within thy gates**."_ (Exodus 20:10)

The stranger was not exempt from Sabbath observanceâ€”the stranger was **included** in it. The blessing of holy rest was extended to all within Israel's gates regardless of ethnic origin.

### The Manna Test: Jews and Gentiles Alike

Before Sinai, God tested the entire congregation regarding His Sabbath:

> _"Behold, I will rain bread from heaven for you; and the people shall go out and gather a certain rate every day, that I may prove them, whether they will walk in my law, or no."_ (Exodus 16:4)

Notice: God said "my law"â€”the law already existed. The Sabbath test came "on the sixth day" when the people were to gather twice as much (verse 5). Some disobeyed:

> _"And it came to pass, that there went out some of the people on the seventh day for to gather, and they found none. And the LORD said unto Moses, How long refuse ye to keep my commandments and my laws?"_ (Exodus 16:27, 28)

The result: "So the people rested on the seventh day" (verse 30).

This occurred at least thirty days before Sinai. The "mixed multitude"â€”Egyptians and other Gentilesâ€”were included among "the whole congregation" that murmured, was tested, was rebuked, and finally obeyed. Jew and Gentile alike were proved by the Sabbath and expected to keep it.

## Isaiah's Sabbath Promise to the Stranger

The prophet Isaiah delivers one of Scripture's most beautiful and explicit promises regarding Gentiles and the Sabbath:

> _"Blessed is the man that doeth this, and the son of man that layeth hold on it; that keepeth the sabbath from polluting it, and keepeth his hand from doing any evil."_ (Isaiah 56:2)

Notice the universal language: "the man"â€”any man, not specifically a Jewish man. But Isaiah goes further, directly addressing Gentile concerns:

> _"Neither let the son of the stranger, that hath joined himself to the LORD, speak, saying, The LORD hath utterly separated me from his people."_ (Isaiah 56:3)

How tender is our God! He anticipates the fear of Gentile converts who might feel excluded, who might wonder if they truly belong to His people. His answer is emphatic:

> _"Also the sons of the stranger, that join themselves to the LORD, to serve him, and to love the name of the LORD, to be his servants, every one that keepeth the sabbath from polluting it, and taketh hold of my covenant; even them will I bring to my holy mountain, and make them joyful in my house of prayer... for mine house shall be called an house of prayer for all people. What glorious promises! The Gentile who keeps the Sabbath:

- Will be made joyful in God's house of prayer

## The Apostolic Church and Gentile Sabbath-Keeping

### Paul's Practice

The apostolic church consistently worshipped on the seventh-day Sabbath, and Gentiles were active participants:

**At Antioch in Pisidia**, Paul addressed "Men of Israel, and ye that fear God" (Acts 13:16). The "God-fearers" were Gentiles who worshipped Israel's God without full conversion to Judaism. After Paul's sermon:

> _"And when the Jews were gone out of the synagogue, the Gentiles besought that these words might be preached to them the next sabbath."_ (Acts 13:42)

If Sunday had replaced the Sabbath, this was the perfect opportunity to say, "Come tomorrowâ€”we worship on the first day now." Instead: "And the next sabbath day came almost the whole city together to hear the word of God" (verse 44).

**At Philippi**, a Gentile city and Roman colony, Paul found no synagogue. What did he do?

> _"And on the sabbath we went out of the city by a river side, where prayer was wont to be made; and we sat down, and spake unto the women which resorted thither."_ (Acts 16:13)

Even without a synagogue, the Sabbath was the day of worship and ministry.

**At Corinth**, Paul maintained his Sabbath practice for extended periods:

> _"And he reasoned in the synagogue every sabbath, and persuaded the Jews and the Greeks."_ (Acts 18:4)

"Greeks" here refers to Gentiles. Week after week, Sabbath after Sabbath, Jews and Gentiles together heard the gospel. Paul continued this pattern for "a year and six months" (verse 11)â€”78 consecutive Sabbaths with both Jews and Greeks.

[Q] **"Wasn't Paul just meeting in the synagogue to reach Jews? Didn't he teach Gentiles on different days?"**

The text explicitly states Paul "persuaded the Jews and the Greeks" **in the synagogue** and **on the Sabbath**. If the Sabbath were merely a Jewish institution, and if Sunday were the Christian day of worship, surely Luke would have mentioned Paul's separate meetings with Gentile believers on Sunday. The silence of Scripture on Sunday worship, combined with the consistent pattern of Sabbath worship for all believers, speaks powerfully.

### James and the Jerusalem Council

At the Jerusalem Council (Acts 15), when the question arose about Gentile converts and the law, James made a remarkable statement:

> _"For Moses of old time hath in every city them that preach him, being read in the synagogues every sabbath day."_ (Acts 15:21)

James expected Gentile converts to continue hearing Moses read in the synagogues on Sabbath. The moral lawâ€”including the Sabbathâ€”was to be taught; only the ceremonial requirements specific to the old covenant were not to be imposed on Gentiles.

## Principles and Great Controversy Themes

### The Universal Scope of God's Law

The great controversy between Christ and Satan centers on God's law. Satan has worked through the centuries to undermine, change, or abolish the law of God. One of his most effective strategies has been to convince Christians that portions of the Ten Commandments were "Jewish" or temporary.

But consider: the Sabbath commandment is the only one that begins with "Remember." It is the seal of God's law, containing His name (LORD), His title (Creator), and His dominion (heaven and earth). It identifies the true God and distinguishes Him from all false gods. To remove the Sabbath from the Gentile church is to remove the very foundation of true worship.

The prophet Daniel foretold a power that would "think to change times and laws" (Daniel 7:25). History has witnessed this prophecy fulfilled in the substitution of Sunday for the Bible Sabbathâ€”not by divine authority, but by human tradition.

### The Sabbath and Righteousness by Faith

The Sabbath is intimately connected with righteousness by faith. When we cease from our own works on the Sabbath, we demonstrate our trust in God's completed workâ€”both in creation and in redemption. The Sabbath is a sign that God sanctifies us (Ezekiel 20:12), making us holy not by our efforts but by His grace.

Hebrews 4 develops this theme beautifully, showing that "there remaineth therefore a rest [*sabbatismos*â€”a Sabbath-keeping] to the people of God" (Hebrews 4:9). The believer who enters God's rest by faith also "hath ceased from his own works, as God did from his" (verse 10). True Sabbath-keeping is an expression of faithâ€”we rest because God has finished His work for us.

This rest is available to Jew and Gentile alike, for "whosoever will" may come.

### Sanctuary Connections

In the earthly sanctuary, the Sabbath was central to Israel's worship. The showbread was changed "every sabbath" (Leviticus 24:8). The Sabbath offerings were prescribed (Numbers 28:9-10). The sanctuary services were ordered around the weekly cycle established at creation.

In the heavenly sanctuary, Christ ministers before the ark of the covenant, which contains the Ten Commandmentsâ€”including the fourth. John saw this ark in vision: "And the temple of God was opened in heaven, and there was seen in his temple the ark of his testament" (Revelation 11:19). The law within that ark has never been changed. The Sabbath still stands as God's holy day for all mankind.
